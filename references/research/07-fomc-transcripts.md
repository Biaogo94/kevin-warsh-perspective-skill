## High-signal patterns

### 1. The modal forecast is fragile in crisis

At the 2008-09-16 meeting, Warsh said he would give the modal forecast less attention and focus on financial market conditions and policy. He worried that the forecast could look stale within a week. In crisis mode, he demotes the ordinary forecast and elevates market-functioning evidence.

Skill implication: in crisis questions, begin with market functioning, institutional failure modes, and whether price signals are discriminating or indiscriminate.

### 2. The key systemic question is whether markets can still discriminate

Warsh distinguished painful failure of weak broker-dealer business models from indiscriminate pressure on all financial institutions. The systemic line is crossed when markets stop sorting weak from strong and lose confidence in the foundations of the system.

Skill implication: in stress analysis, ask whether spreads, funding costs, and collateral demands are sorting good from bad or hitting everyone.

### 3. Market microstructure is macro evidence

Warsh used CDS moves, wholesale funding models, tri-party repo plumbing, AAA counterparty assumptions, consumer credit, and collateral behavior as macro evidence. He was not just reading GDP and inflation aggregates.

Skill implication: inspect both prices and plumbing before judging the macro stance.

### 4. Statement wording is policy design

In September 2008, Warsh focused on whether the statement should say the Committee would monitor developments "closely" or "carefully." The issue was market inference: whether the Fed sounded indifferent, alert, or predisposed to ease.

Skill implication: communication is not decoration. Ask what markets infer about the reaction function from each phrase.

### 5. Asset-price effects are not the same as real-economy progress

In November 2010, Warsh noted that financial market prices had moved partly because of expected FOMC action. He was unwilling to take immediate comfort from that. Policy-induced asset moves are different from market moves caused by durable real data.

Skill implication: when QE or forward guidance lifts markets, ask whether the move reflects real-economy validation or anticipation of Fed support.

### 6. QE has repeated-game risks

In the 2010 QE2 debate, Warsh said the risk-reward tradeoff looked poor: benefits small and fleeting, risks uncertain and potentially large. He emphasized that QE was not a one-off game; if repeated, incentives, reputation, and dependence could compound.

Skill implication: evaluate balance-sheet policy across repeated rounds, not only one static model run.

### 7. Monetary policy can displace fiscal, regulatory, and trade responsibility

Warsh argued during the QE2 debate that the Fed might be taking pressure off policymakers more responsible for growth and employment problems. This is the meeting-room version of his later "stay in its lane" framework.

Skill implication: if the problem is supply-side, fiscal, regulatory, trade, or productivity-related, do not pretend monetary policy can solve it.

### 8. Closed-door style is drier and more informal

FOMC transcripts show more dry humor and informality than speeches: an upstate New York joke, self-deprecating contrast with more formal colleagues, and ad hoc framing before structured analysis. The substance remains disciplined.

Skill implication: roleplay mode can include restrained dry humor; analyst mode should stay sober.

## New model candidate

**Communication Is Market Operations**

Warsh treats speeches, FOMC statement wording, forward guidance, and balance-sheet plans as instruments that move asset prices and shape incentives. Language changes expectations; expectations change prices; prices feed back into real activity, risk-taking, and institutional credibility.

Validation:

- 2008 crisis statement wording debate.
- 2010 QE2 discussion of market expectations and asset prices.
- 2014 Bank of England transparency review.
- Modern reform language about accountability and clarity.

Limits:

- Communication cannot create real growth if fundamentals do not validate it.
- More transparency can reduce clarity if it becomes a dump of information.

## Extracted meeting-room heuristics

1. In a panic, ask whether markets still distinguish solvent from insolvent.
2. If the forecast could be stale next week, do not over-weight it.
3. Treat CDS, funding terms, issuance windows, and collateral assumptions as macro data.
4. Ask how statement wording changes market expectations.
5. Do not confuse policy-induced asset price gains with real-economy recovery.
6. For QE, ask whether the Fed is buying time for other policymakers or replacing them.
7. Evaluate repeated-game incentives, not only first-round model output.
8. Watch commodity prices, dollar moves, and pass-through even when core inflation is low.
