# Kevin Warsh research: decisions, actions, and institutional behavior

Research cutoff: 2026-06-02.

## Decision record

### 2006-2011: Federal Reserve Board governor

Warsh served through the financial crisis and early recovery. He supported extraordinary liquidity and market-functioning interventions during panic conditions. Later remarks distinguish these crisis actions from prolonged post-crisis accommodation.

Decision inference: willing to use overwhelming central bank force in genuine emergencies, but wants exit discipline afterward.

### 2008 crisis: liquidity first, but not permanent state finance

Warsh frames the Fed's crisis role as indispensable in unusual and exigent circumstances. His later speeches draw a boundary between liquidity provision and fiscal capital allocation. He thinks the Fed should go to the edge of authority in a panic, then return to a narrower role.

Decision inference: ask "is this liquidity, capital, or fiscal support?" before approving central bank action.

Transcript refinement: in the 2008-09-16 FOMC meeting, Warsh's crisis analysis focused on whether market stress was discriminating among weak institutions or becoming indiscriminate across the foundations of the financial system. He watched CDS spreads, wholesale funding models, tri-party repo plumbing, AAA counterparty assumptions, and AIG-like spillovers. This shows that his market-dashboard model is microstructure-heavy, not just macro-financial.

### 2010 QE2 skepticism

In "Rejecting the Requiem", Warsh describes QE2 as limited, circumscribed, and subject to review. He warns that an expanding balance sheet is not a free option, can distort Treasury price signals, and can create global spillovers.

Decision inference: nontraditional tools require explicit thresholds, benefits tests, and risk monitoring.

Transcript refinement: in the 2010-11-03 FOMC meeting, Warsh argued that the risk-reward tradeoff for QE2 was poor: benefits looked small and fleeting while risks were uncertain and potentially large. He focused on repeated-game consequences, fiscal-policy displacement, commodity-price pass-through, dollar effects, and the possibility that the Fed was trying to make asset prices do work that real economic policy should do.

### 2008-2010: statement language as policy design

FOMC transcripts show Warsh paying close attention to statement wording. In September 2008, he pushed for language that showed watchful attention to financial stress without implying a predisposition to immediate easing. In November 2010, he worried that statement language about purchase reviews might define the reaction function too narrowly.

Decision inference: when drafting communications, ask how each phrase moves expectations and whether it narrows or broadens the Fed's future options.

### 2010 regulation reform stance

In "Regulation and Its Discontents", he supports better resolution authority but argues it is not enough. He wants market discipline, comparable disclosure, competition, and credible failure, not permanent state-client banks.

Decision inference: prefer rules and market discipline over discretionary micromanagement.

### 2014 Bank of England transparency review

Warsh reviewed the Bank of England MPC's transparency practices and recommended a balance between accountability and genuine deliberation. This shows a preference for institutional design that improves accountability without turning committee deliberations into theater.

Decision inference: communication reform should make policy more understandable and accountable, not merely louder.

### 2026 Fed chair nomination and confirmation

Warsh's 2026 testimony makes independence, mission focus, price stability, and accountability the frame for his chairmanship. He pledges to keep monetary policy independent while working with Congress and the Administration on non-monetary matters within the Fed's remit.

Decision inference: expect reform efforts around framework review, communication, balance-sheet policy, and Fed-Treasury boundaries.

## Decision heuristics extracted

1. If it is a panic, stabilize market functioning decisively.
2. If it is not a panic, do not normalize emergency tools.
3. If inflation persists, do not outsource blame to relative-price shocks.
4. If the balance sheet is large, treat it as policy and inspect its inflation and market-price effects.
5. If the Fed's credibility is at risk, narrow the mission rather than expand it.
6. If a firm is too big to fail, the architecture is not yet credible.
7. If communication is frequent but confusing, reduce noise and improve accountability.
8. If models and markets diverge, inspect the market signal before dismissing it.
9. If statement language changes, ask what market behavior it will induce.
10. If QE works mainly through asset prices, test whether those prices are validating real growth or only front-running Fed support.

## Key tension

Warsh wants strong central-bank action in crisis and a much smaller central-bank footprint in normal times. The hard part is defining the boundary in real time.
