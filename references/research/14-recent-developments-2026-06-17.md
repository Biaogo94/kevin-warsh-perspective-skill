## Skill implications

### 1. Same-day official-source boundary

When the prompt concerns a live FOMC day, separate:

- confirmed official documents: statement, implementation note, SEP, press conference transcript/video, minutes;
- official schedule: meeting date and release times;
- market expectations: rate probabilities, analyst previews, predicted dot-plot changes;
- press commentary: speculation about tone, communication reform, or political dynamics.

Do not state that the FOMC "held", "cut", "hiked", "removed dots", or changed language until the official release is available or a reliable live report clearly says the release occurred.

### 2. Energy-shock propagation test

The May 2026 CPI mix is exactly where a Warsh-style skill can get lazy if it only says "inflation is high." The better test is:

1. Is the shock concentrated in energy/gasoline, or broadening into core services and wages?
2. Are inflation expectations and breakevens drifting?
3. Are firms and workers building the shock into pricing and wage-setting?
4. Does the Fed risk validating headline inflation by easing too soon?
5. Does real-side weakness justify a rate move, or is the correct posture patience plus credibility?

Warsh-style answer:

- Energy is a relative price shock at first.
- It becomes a monetary-policy credibility problem if it propagates into expectations, wages, core services, or broad pricing behavior.
- A high headline number with contained core inflation argues against panic hikes, but also against politically convenient cuts unless broader data justify them.

### 3. Communication-reform watchlist

Do not overstate reform before it happens. Watch:

- statement length and wording;
- whether the statement keeps, removes, or changes easing-bias language;
- SEP and dot plot participation or design;
- press conference frequency, tone, and use of uncertainty language;
- whether Warsh uses "less communication" to clarify the reaction function or to create opacity.

The skill's current "Communication Is Market Operations" model remains right, but it should now explicitly treat reduced communication as a policy design choice with tradeoffs.

### 4. Chair-mode calibration

Warsh's first FOMC is more likely to show sequencing than revolution. Chair mode should assume:

- early continuity where abrupt change would create avoidable market surprise;
- gradual reform of communication architecture;
- strong inflation-credibility framing if headline inflation is high;
- attention to consensus management inside a divided Committee;
- strict separation of political demands for cuts from mandate-based policy.

## Recommended skill update

Add two small heuristics:

1. **Respect the release clock.** On FOMC days, do not convert previews into outcomes. Use official documents once released.
2. **Run the propagation test.** For energy/tariff/supply shocks, decide whether the shock is still relative-price movement or has become self-reinforcing inflation.

These updates strengthen the skill without changing its core models.
