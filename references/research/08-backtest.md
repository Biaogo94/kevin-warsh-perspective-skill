# Kevin Warsh skill backtest

Research cutoff: 2026-06-02.

Purpose: test whether the Warsh perspective skill would have predicted Warsh's later public behavior using only facts available before or at the event. This is not a statistical forecast; it is a qualitative validation of the skill's mental models.

## Method

For each case:

1. Provide the event context and economic/market facts available at the time.
2. Run the skill's decision logic:
   - mandate first
   - market dashboard
   - crisis boundary
   - balance-sheet realism
   - communication-as-policy
   - fiscal/regulatory responsibility
3. Compare with Warsh's actual FOMC vote, statement-language behavior, or later public remarks.

Scores:

- Strong match: predicts both stance and reasoning.
- Partial match: predicts stance but misses emphasis, or predicts reasoning but not vote.
- Miss: materially wrong.

## Case 1: 2008-09-16 FOMC, Lehman/AIG turmoil

### Facts available at the time

- Lehman had failed; Merrill had agreed to be sold; AIG was under acute stress.
- Broker-dealer CDS spreads and equity prices were moving violently.
- The federal funds target was 2 percent.
- Inflation had recently been elevated, but dollar strength and falling commodity prices were easing near-term inflation pressure.
- The central question was whether to cut rates immediately or hold while acknowledging financial stress.

### Skill prediction

A Warsh-style read should:

- Demote the modal forecast because market architecture is breaking.
- Focus on whether markets are discriminating among weak firms or losing confidence in the foundations of the system.
- Support language that flags financial market stress and preserves optionality.
- Be open to future easing if financial conditions tighten materially and persistently.
- Likely vote to hold at 2 percent if the immediate evidence is still unclear, while strengthening the statement.

### Actual Warsh behavior

Strong match.

Warsh said he would give the modal forecast "short shrift" and focus on financial market conditions and policy. He analyzed whether market pressure was limited to weak business models or becoming indiscriminate across institutions. He supported alternative B, holding the funds rate at 2 percent, but proposed statement language highlighting financial market strains and monitoring developments carefully.

### Validation note

This validates the skill's crisis-boundary model and communication-as-policy model.

## Case 2: 2008-10-28/29 FOMC, worsening credit conditions

### Facts available at the time

- Credit markets were illiquid and volatile.
- Economic data after mid-September were worsening.
- Inflation pressure had eased enough to create policy flexibility.
- The Committee was considering another rate cut.

### Skill prediction

A Warsh-style read should:

- Be less optimistic than the forecast if market and corporate data show a sharper deterioration.
- Accept that the problem is not principally monetary policy, but that monetary policy is still relevant at the margin.
- Protect institutional credibility and avoid being cornered into serial cuts without explaining the lower bound.
- Likely support a cut, while emphasizing that credibility and communication matter more than the exact basis-point move.

### Actual Warsh behavior

Strong match.

Warsh said market prices and data showed another leg down, expected prolonged credit strain, and said the problem was not principally monetary policy. He still supported alternative A. He emphasized that the FOMC's most valuable asset was credibility, not the remaining basis points of policy room, and said the Fed needed to tell the lower-bound story before the story was told for it.

### Validation note

This validates the skill's "marginal policy relevance, institutional credibility first" behavior.

## Case 3: 2008-12-15/16 FOMC, zero lower bound

### Facts available at the time

- The recession was accelerating and global deterioration was broadening.
- The effective funds rate was already near zero.
- The Committee had to decide how to state the zero-rate range and whether to emphasize balance-sheet/open-market operations.
- Market functioning remained fragile.

### Skill prediction

A Warsh-style read should:

- Recognize that rate policy is constrained but not irrelevant.
- Avoid overstating what a zero-rate decision can accomplish.
- Support forceful action because this is still crisis mode.
- Emphasize market functioning, balance-sheet operations, and communication clarity.
- Likely vote yes with the Committee, while watching wording closely.

### Actual Warsh behavior

Strong match.

Warsh said the zero-lower-bound discussion had two risks: overstating the importance of that day's judgment and understating the importance of prospective Fed actions. He argued the protracted weakness was not simply about monetary stimulus but about broken financial architecture. He supported action, participated in wording discussions, and voted yes.

### Validation note

This validates the skill's distinction between "not enough by itself" and "still must act in crisis."

## Case 4: 2010-08-10 FOMC, reinvestment decision

### Facts available at the time

- Recovery had slowed; employment gains were weak.
- Prepayments threatened to shrink the Fed's balance sheet passively.
- Deflation risk was discussed but inflation expectations remained anchored.
- The Committee considered reinvesting principal payments to keep holdings from declining.

### Skill prediction

A Warsh-style read should:

- Push back on panic narratives that the economy's long-term potential had suddenly changed.
- Be skeptical that deflation risk is dominant if commodity prices, dollar moves, and expectations do not confirm it.
- Treat passive balance-sheet shrinkage as an unintended tightening.
- Support reinvestment if framed as preventing de facto tightening, not as a large new initiative.
- Demand careful messaging and optionality about duration and asset type.

### Actual Warsh behavior

Strong match.

Warsh said he had been less optimistic than the Tealbook but did not think recent data fundamentally changed his view. He pushed back on deflation risk by citing commodity prices, anchored expectations, the dollar, and price measures. He supported keeping holdings constant, preferred market-based flexibility in whether to reinvest in Treasuries or agency MBS, opposed locking in duration, and worked on wording such as "keep constant" rather than vague "maintain."

### Validation note

This case is especially important because a simplistic "Warsh is anti-balance-sheet" skill would fail. The actual pattern is conditional: avoid de facto tightening, but frame and bound the action carefully.

## Case 5: 2010-11-02/03 FOMC, QE2

### Facts available at the time

- Recovery was sluggish and unemployment high.
- Core inflation was low, but commodity prices, dollar moves, import prices, and business anecdotes raised questions about 2011 price pressures.
- Markets had rallied in anticipation of FOMC action.
- The Committee considered a large Treasury purchase program.

### Skill prediction

A Warsh-style read should:

- Treat policy-induced asset-price gains differently from real-data-driven improvement.
- Ask whether financial markets are being asked to drag the real economy.
- Be skeptical of QE if benefits look small, fleeting, or dependent on repeated interventions.
- Argue that fiscal, regulatory, and trade policymakers should carry more of the growth burden.
- Likely vote against or reluctantly dissent if the program is large and framed as a major easing initiative.

### Actual Warsh behavior

Partial match.

Warsh's reasoning matched strongly. He said he was out of step with most of the Committee, that the path was not his preferred path, that the burden was being moved away from other policymakers, and that the risk-reward tradeoff looked poor. He argued markets had moved because of expected FOMC action, and was skeptical that asset prices could do the real economy's hard work. However, he ultimately voted yes for the program after voicing his countervailing view.

### Validation note

This is the most important miss. The skill predicted a likely dissent too strongly. Actual Warsh behavior shows an added model: **institutional consensus discipline**. He may voice sharp internal disagreement but still vote with the Committee when the Chair has assembled consensus and the policy includes review/conditionality.

## Skill update required

Add an eighth decision heuristic or caution:

**Consensus discipline:** Do not predict dissent solely from sharp disagreement. Warsh may state a countervailing view strongly but vote with the Committee if the policy has conditionality, review language, and the Chair's consensus is clear. Predict dissent only when the policy crosses a hard boundary, removes review/optionality, or makes the Fed a substitute for fiscal authority without limits.

This adjustment improves future predictions without weakening the core QE skepticism model.
