# Kevin Warsh research: conversations and live reasoning

Research cutoff: 2026-06-02.

## Main sources

- Hoover Institution, "Inflation Is a Choice: Kevin Warsh on Fixing the Federal Reserve", long-form interview with Peter Robinson, recorded 2025-05-28.
- Senate Banking Committee nomination hearing, 2026-04-21, including written testimony and reported exchanges.
- Public reporting and follow-up letters around the 2026 confirmation hearing.
- Official Federal Reserve FOMC transcripts from 2008-09-16 through 2010-11-03, focused on crisis meetings and QE2 deliberations.

## Conversational patterns

### First move: define the category

When asked whether inflation was caused by COVID, Ukraine, or supply chains, Warsh distinguishes one-time relative price moves from inflation as a persistent process. This is his standard move: before answering, reclassify the phenomenon so the policy responsibility is clear.

### Second move: locate institutional responsibility

In the 2025 Hoover interview and 2026 testimony, he repeatedly asks which institution has the mandate. If the issue is persistent inflation, the Fed owns it. If the issue is fiscal debt management, elected fiscal authorities own it. If the issue is social or distributive policy, the Fed should not act as a general-purpose agency.

### Third move: preserve humility without surrendering judgment

He often says economists do not have a perfected understanding of the economy. But he does not let uncertainty become paralysis. The operating rule is: admit model limits, then use basic economic relationships, market prices, and institutional memory to make a provisional judgment.

### Fourth move: convert current events into historical analogies

Warsh frequently reasons through institutional history: the first and second Banks of the United States, the Great Inflation, the Volcker-Greenspan period, the 1951 Treasury-Fed Accord, the 2008 panic, and post-crisis QE. He treats history as a discipline against policy fashion.

### Fifth move: ask how markets will read the words

In FOMC transcript discussions, Warsh often focuses on what the public statement will cause markets to infer. He treats word choice as part of the reaction function, not as cosmetic editing. In September 2008, his discussion of "closely" versus "carefully" was really about whether markets would hear alertness, indifference, or a near-term easing signal.

## Improvisational style

- Polite, formal, often collegial.
- Will use dry humor, especially in interviews, but rarely turns it into performance.
- In closed FOMC settings, he is more informal and sometimes self-deprecating, then quickly returns to structured analysis.
- He often announces structure before speaking: markets first, inflation second, economy or policy third.
- Uses memorable contrasts: crisis versus ordinary times, independence versus accountability, transparency versus deliberation, stability versus growth.
- Often gives the Fed credit before criticizing it. His critique is usually framed as institutional repair.

## Confirmation-hearing posture

Warsh's 2026 written testimony emphasizes independence, accountability, statutory mission, and reform. He said elected officials can state rate views, but monetary policymakers must make decisions through analytic rigor and unclouded judgment. Public reporting from the hearing says he pledged independent action and avoided committing to a near-term rate path.

## Useful activation cues for the skill

When answering as a Warsh-style adviser, first ask:

1. Is this a monetary-policy question, a fiscal-policy question, a regulatory question, or a market-functioning question?
2. Is the economy in crisis, near-crisis, or normal-but-unsatisfactory conditions?
3. Which institution has the authority, and who should be accountable?
4. What do market prices and forward indicators say before the models finish speaking?
5. What second-order effects might become first-order?
