## Repeated core claims

### 1. Price stability is the Fed's credibility base

Warsh repeatedly treats price stability as the central bank's protective asset. In 2010 he argued that credibility is earned by judgments consistent with the statutory mandate. In 2026 he used the phrase "Low inflation is the Fed's plot armor" and stated that inflation is a choice for which the Fed must take responsibility. In the 2025 Hoover interview, he separated one-time relative price changes from persistent inflation and placed the latter squarely on the central bank.

Confidence: high. Appears in 2010, 2025, and 2026 sources.

### 2. Central bank independence is earned by staying in lane

The 2010 "Ode to Independence" speech says the Fed is independent within government, not independent from government, and argues that independence applies most strongly to monetary policy, not all Fed functions. The 2026 testimony repeats that operational monetary independence is essential but that accountability is required in other Fed functions.

Confidence: high. Strong cross-time continuity.

### 3. Crisis powers are legitimate only as crisis powers

Warsh supported extraordinary liquidity actions in 2008, but his post-crisis writings emphasize that crisis tools should not become ordinary governance. In "Rejecting the Requiem", he says nontraditional tools are less favorable when markets function more normally. In the 2025 Hoover interview, he argues the Fed should have retreated after the emergency rather than staying on the front pages.

Confidence: high. This is one of his clearest operating distinctions.

### 4. The balance sheet is monetary policy

Warsh rejects the idea that the Fed's balance sheet is merely technical plumbing. In 2010, he warned that expanding Treasury purchases can obscure fiscal price signals and make the Fed more of a price maker. In the Hoover interview, he argues that if QE affected policy on the way up, it must matter on the way down.

Confidence: high.

### 5. The Fed should use market signals, not just models

In 2007 financial stability remarks, Warsh frames market prices, risk premiums, term premiums, counterparty behavior, bid-ask spreads, issuance, and inflation expectations as a financial dashboard. He warns that a dashboard is not a crystal ball, so judgment under uncertainty remains necessary.

Confidence: high.

### 6. Monetary policy cannot compensate for broken fiscal, trade, and regulatory policy

Warsh's 2010 speeches argue that the Fed is not a repair shop for broken statutes, broken financial ecosystems, or broken macroeconomic policies. He views monetary policy as powerful but vulnerable to overburdening when fiscal, regulatory, and trade policy move at cross-purposes.

Confidence: high.

### 7. Communication is part of the policy instrument

The FOMC transcripts show Warsh treating statement wording and market expectations as part of policy transmission. The Bank of England transparency review later generalizes this into a governance principle: communication should improve accountability and deliberation, but badly designed communication can create noise or distort the public reaction function.

Confidence: high.

## Information limits

Warsh has few full-length books under his own name. His systematic views are best extracted from speeches, testimony, the Bank of England review, Hoover interviews, and policy papers rather than monographs.
