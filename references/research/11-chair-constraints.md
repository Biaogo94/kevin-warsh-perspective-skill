# Chair constraints and role-mode adjustments

Research cutoff: 2026-06-02.

Purpose: prevent the skill from overusing Warsh's outside-critic language when the prompt asks about actions as Federal Reserve chair. A chair is not a columnist, not a regional bank dissenter, and not a solitary governor. The role changes the expression, the coalition problem, and the timing of action.

## Three role modes

### Mode 1: Governor inside the FOMC

Likely behavior:

- Speak candidly in the room.
- Use market evidence and institutional-risk arguments.
- Press on statement language and optionality.
- Vote with the Committee more often than the rhetoric alone would predict.

Use when:

- Backtesting 2006-2011 FOMC behavior.
- Modeling internal deliberation or likely private objections.

Do not:

- Infer public dissent from private disagreement.

### Mode 2: Outside reformer / Hoover-style critic

Likely behavior:

- State failures more sharply.
- Emphasize Fed reform, price stability, balance-sheet excess, and overreach.
- Attribute growth weakness partly to fiscal, regulatory, or structural policy failures outside the Fed.
- Use broader institutional history and public persuasion.

Use when:

- Analyzing speeches, interviews, op-eds, testimony, or reform proposals.
- Asking what Warsh would criticize from outside office.

Do not:

- Assume the same sharpness would appear verbatim in an FOMC statement as chair.

### Mode 3: Chair of the Federal Reserve Board

Likely behavior:

- Protect the institution while changing its direction.
- Build internal consensus before changing the public reaction function.
- Avoid gratuitous surprises; use speeches, minutes, SEP, and press conferences to prepare markets.
- Separate monetary-policy independence from accountability to Congress.
- Preserve optionality and avoid promising a precise rate path.
- Make reform sound like restoration of mandate and credibility, not personal combat.

Use when:

- The user asks "what will the Fed do," "as chair," "current Fed chair," or asks about live FOMC strategy.

Do not:

- Make him sound like an outside critic issuing unilateral orders.
- Predict a dramatic public rupture unless a policy crosses a hard institutional boundary.

## Chair-specific decision constraints

### Consensus constraint

The chair must count votes, sequence changes, and give the Committee a shared language. Even a strongly held view may emerge as gradual statement wording, a speech, a review process, or balance-sheet normalization plan rather than a one-meeting break.

Use this especially after the 2010 QE2 backtest: sharp objection did not equal dissent.

### Market-surprise constraint

As chair, a Warsh-style actor would still believe that words are market operations. If the goal is institutional credibility, surprise should be reserved for true emergencies or necessary corrections, not for theatrical toughness.

### Independence constraint

The chair must defend operational monetary-policy independence while accepting oversight. The formulation is:

- The Fed is independent in monetary policy decisions.
- The Fed is accountable to Congress for its mandate, tools, governance, and transparency.
- The Fed should avoid becoming an instrument of fiscal policy or White House preference.
- Regular information exchange with Treasury is compatible with independence when statutory roles and policy authority remain delineated. Use a wide informational lens and a narrow policy remit.

### Tool-scope constraint

As chair, instrument separation matters more:

- Rates: ordinary monetary-policy tool.
- Balance sheet: policy tool with fiscal and market-structure effects.
- Liquidity facilities: market-functioning tool for stress, not growth policy.
- Supervision: safety-and-soundness and resolvability, not industrial policy.
- Fiscal policy: Congress and Treasury, not the Fed.

### Communication constraint

The chair must make the Committee's reaction function legible without burying it in words. More pages, more projections, or more speeches do not automatically improve transparency.

Reduced communication is itself a policy design choice. It can clarify the reaction function if it removes false precision and mechanical forward guidance; it can damage accountability if it merely withholds useful information. In chair mode, evaluate statement wording, SEP/dot-plot choices, press conference cadence, and market interpretation together.

Observed calibration from 2026-06-17:

- Warsh shortened the statement and removed the prior easing bias and forward guidance.
- He encouraged other SEP submissions but withheld his own projection.
- He treated dots as conditional forecasts rather than promises.

Do not collapse these choices into "less transparency." Test whether current facts, uncertainty, and responsibility remain clear while false precision is reduced.

### First-meeting constraint

At the first FOMC meeting as chair, avoid predicting revolution unless official documents show it. A Warsh-style chair can signal reform by sequencing: modest statement changes, a more conditional press conference, less enthusiasm for the dot plot, and a clearer inflation-credibility frame. Abrupt changes are more likely when market functioning or credibility requires them.

Observed result: policy continuity and framework reform occurred together. Rates were held unanimously, while communications changed immediately and five task forces were launched. Update the prior expectation from "modest statement changes" to "rate continuity can coexist with rapid process and communication reform."

### Task-force governance constraint

Warsh's first-chair behavior used independent task forces for communications, balance sheet, data sources, productivity/jobs, and inflation frameworks. In chair mode:

- treat task forces as structured agenda-setting, not final policy;
- expect internal staff plus outside expertise;
- distinguish the chair's questions from the Committee's eventual answers;
- watch implementation dates, votes, and operating directives before calling a reform complete.

### Consensus-as-output constraint

A unanimous decision can be an important chair achievement. Do not infer that 12-0 means no internal disagreement. Evaluate what language, optionality, and process allowed the Committee to agree.

## Political-pressure response model

When faced with White House pressure to cut rates:

1. Acknowledge elected officials' right to express views.
2. Re-anchor the Fed's job in price stability, maximum employment, and law.
3. Say policy will be based on data, outlook, risk balance, and the mandate.
4. Avoid personal counterattacks.
5. Protect the distinction between monetary independence and democratic accountability.

Warsh-style sentence pattern:

- "The Fed earns its independence by doing the job Congress assigned, not by entering the political debate."
- "Public officials can express views; the Committee must make judgments."
- "The best answer to pressure is performance."

## How chair mode changes predictions

Rate path:

- More conditional and less dramatic than outside commentary may imply.
- Credibility first when inflation is persistent.
- Willing to ease if inflation progress is durable and labor/market risks justify it.
- On live FOMC days, separate pre-meeting expectations from official outcomes. Do not treat analyst previews as FOMC decisions.

Balance sheet:

- More likely to frame reform as normalization, transparency, and better operating framework.
- Less likely to announce abrupt runoff if Treasury-market functioning is fragile.

Financial crisis:

- Decisive action if systemic functioning breaks.
- Stronger exit language after the immediate panic.

Regulation:

- Emphasize capital, liquidity, disclosure, and resolution.
- Avoid making large banks permanent state clients.

Communication:

- More disciplined, less polemical, and more committee-centered than Hoover interviews.
- "Less communication" should mean clearer communication, not unexplained opacity.

## Failure modes to avoid

- Outside-critic overhang: using 2025 interview rhetoric as if it were an FOMC statement.
- Hawk caricature: predicting tight policy regardless of incoming inflation and labor data.
- Anti-QE caricature: ignoring that emergency balance-sheet action can be legitimate.
- Dissent inflation: predicting no votes too easily.
- Independence absolutism: treating congressional oversight as illegitimate.
- Market worship: taking market prices as final truth rather than a dashboard requiring judgment.
